Locomotion and Balance

Learning Objectives​

  • Understand the biomechanics of human and animal locomotion
  • Learn the fundamental principles of balance control in dynamic systems
  • Explore various locomotion strategies for humanoid robots
  • Analyze the role of sensory feedback in maintaining balance
  • Study control algorithms for locomotion and balance

Introduction to Locomotion​

Locomotion is the ability to move from one place to another. In humanoid robotics, this involves generating coordinated movement patterns that enable the robot to walk, run, or navigate various terrains. Humanoid locomotion systems must be robust, energy-efficient, and capable of adapting to environmental changes.

Biomechanics of Human Locomotion​

Human locomotion is a complex process involving the coordination of multiple body segments, muscles, and neural control systems. Key aspects include:

Walking Gait Cycle​

The human walking gait cycle consists of two main phases:

  • Stance phase (about 60% of the cycle): when the foot is in contact with the ground
  • Swing phase (about 40% of the cycle): when the foot is off the ground and swinging forward

Center of Mass and Zero Moment Point​

The Center of Mass (CoM) is a critical parameter in locomotion. For stable walking, the CoM must be controlled within specific boundaries. The Zero Moment Point (ZMP) is a related concept representing the point on the ground where the net moment of the ground reaction forces is zero. Keeping the ZMP within the support polygon (typically the foot area) is essential for stable locomotion.

Balance Control Strategies​

Balance in humanoid robots can be achieved through several strategies:

Static Balance​

Static balance involves maintaining the center of mass within the support base. This is straightforward when the robot is standing still but becomes challenging during movement.

Dynamic Balance​

Dynamic balance allows the robot to maintain stability through active control during movement. This involves controlling the center of mass and angular momentum trajectories.

Reactive Control​

Reactive control systems respond to disturbances by adjusting the robot's posture or movement in real-time. These systems often use feedback from inertial measurement units (IMUs) and force sensors.

Predictive Control​

Predictive control anticipates potential balance losses and takes preventive actions. This includes adjusting gait parameters or taking a step to recover balance when needed.

Locomotion Techniques​

Inverted Pendulum Model​

The inverted pendulum model treats the humanoid robot as a point mass on a massless leg. This simplified model is useful for understanding basic balance control concepts.

Capture Point​

The capture point indicates where the robot must step to come to rest. Understanding capture point dynamics is essential for implementing balance recovery strategies.

Walking Pattern Generation​

Generating stable walking patterns involves:

  • Trajectory planning for feet, body, and hands
  • Real-time adjustments based on sensor feedback
  • Adaptation to terrain changes and disturbances

Control Algorithms​

Linear Inverted Pendulum Mode (LIPM)​

The Linear Inverted Pendulum Mode is a simplified model that assumes the center of mass moves at a constant height. This leads to the equation:

ẍ = ω²(x - x_zmp)

where ω² = g/h (g is gravity, h is the height of the center of mass above ground), x is the center of mass position, and x_zmp is the zero moment point.

Preview Control​

Preview control uses planned future footsteps to generate stable walking trajectories. This anticipatory approach improves stability and disturbance rejection.

Model Predictive Control (MPC)​

Model Predictive Control optimizes future control actions over a finite horizon while considering system constraints. It's particularly effective for locomotion control due to its ability to handle multiple objectives and constraints.

Sensory Systems for Balance​

Inertial Measurement Units (IMUs)​

IMUs provide information about the robot's orientation and angular velocity. This is crucial for maintaining postural stability and detecting falls.

Force/Torque Sensors​

Force/torque sensors in the feet provide information about the ground reaction forces and moments, enabling precise control of the center of pressure and zero moment point.

Vision Systems​

Vision can help in terrain assessment, obstacle detection, and adjusting locomotion parameters based on environment.

Stability Measures​

Quantifying stability in humanoid robots involves several metrics:

  • Stability margins (distance to edge of support polygon)
  • Energy stability measures
  • Capture point-based stability measures

Challenges in Locomotion and Balance​

Terrain Adaptation​

Humanoid robots must be able to walk on various surfaces including uneven terrain, stairs, and slippery surfaces.

Disturbance Rejection​

Robots must handle external disturbances such as pushes or uneven surfaces while maintaining stable locomotion.

Energy Efficiency​

Achieving human-like energy efficiency remains a significant challenge in humanoid locomotion.

Chapter Summary​

This chapter explored the fundamental concepts of locomotion and balance in humanoid robots. We examined the biomechanics of human locomotion, balance control strategies, and various control algorithms. Understanding these principles is crucial for developing humanoid robots that can move and interact with the environment in a stable and efficient manner.
